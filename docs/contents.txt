MAC 208: Editing and Graphics of Communication (3 Units)
Course Description
This foundational course introduces students to the principles, processes, and professional practices of editing and the application of graphics within communication contexts. It develops students’ capacity to critically assess, refine, and prepare content for publication across print, digital, and broadcast media.

Students will gain practical competence in editing manuscripts for newspapers, magazines, and specialised publications, alongside the strategic use of graphics in advertising and public communication. The course integrates theoretical knowledge with applied skills in design, emphasising clarity, accuracy, aesthetics, and audience engagement.

Key areas of focus include editorial techniques, headline and caption writing, typography, colour theory, layout design, and the integration of visual elements such as photographs and informational graphics. By the end of the course, students are expected to demonstrate proficiency in both textual and visual communication within professional media environments.

SLIDE 8: Evolution of Editing Globally
Story-Led Explanation
Imagine stepping into a printing workshop in the 15th century. The room is dim, the air thick with ink and metal. A printer stands over rows of tiny metal letters, arranging each one by hand. There is no delete key, no spell check, no undo button. Every word must be physically constructed.

This is where editing began, not as a sophisticated profession, but as a necessity. If a single letter was misplaced, the entire meaning could collapse. Proofreading, at this stage, was not about style or clarity. It was about survival of meaning. One mistake could mean reprinting an entire page, at great cost.

This early print era established the first principle of editing: precision matters because errors are expensive.

Now move forward to the 18th and 19th centuries. Newspapers begin to emerge as powerful social institutions. Information is no longer static. It is timely, political, and influential. With this shift comes a new need: someone must take responsibility for what is published.

This is the birth of the editor as a role.

Editors are no longer just correcting errors. They are making decisions:

What is newsworthy?

What should be published?

What should be suppressed?

Editing becomes a position of authority. It moves from the printing table into the centre of public life. The editor becomes a gatekeeper of information and, by extension, a shaper of public opinion.

By the 20th century, journalism evolves into a profession with standards, ethics, and formal training. Newsrooms become structured environments with defined roles: reporters, sub-editors, editors-in-chief.

Editing now operates on multiple levels:

Ensuring factual accuracy

Maintaining organisational style

Protecting legal and ethical standards

This period introduces the idea that editing is not just technical work. It is institutional responsibility. A poorly edited story is no longer just an error. It is a threat to credibility, and potentially, to public trust.

Then we arrive at the digital era.

The newsroom changes completely. Deadlines shrink from days to hours, then to minutes. News is no longer printed once a day. It is updated continuously.

Editing becomes:

Real-time

Multi-platform

Audience-driven

A story may be edited several times after publication. Headlines are adjusted for search engines. Images are optimised for engagement. Readers respond instantly through comments and social media.

The editor is no longer working in isolation. The editor is working in a live, interactive environment.

Key Insight to Emphasise
What began as manual correction of printed text has evolved into a system of continuous, strategic communication management across platforms.

The tools have changed, but the core responsibility remains:

To ensure that what is communicated is accurate, clear, and meaningful.

Memory Anchor for Students
You may summarise the evolution in four stages:

Craft Stage: Fixing physical errors

Control Stage: Deciding what gets published

Professional Stage: Upholding standards and ethics

Digital Stage: Managing speed, platforms, and audience interaction

SLIDE 9: Editing in the Nigerian Context
Story-Led Explanation
Now, bring the story closer to home.

Imagine Nigeria in the late 19th century. The printing press arrives not as a neutral tool, but as an instrument of influence. मिशनary publications begin to circulate, using print to spread religious ideas and Western education.

At this stage, editing serves a very specific purpose: preservation of message. Content is controlled, structured, and aligned with the मिशनary agenda.

But something begins to change.

As the colonial period progresses, Nigerian voices begin to emerge in print. Newspapers become tools of resistance and political awakening. Editing is no longer just about correctness. It becomes a tool of advocacy and nationalism.

Editors are not just professionals. They are activists, intellectuals, and political actors.

They ask:

How do we frame our reality?

How do we challenge colonial narratives?

Editing, in this era, becomes a form of power. The way a story is written and presented can influence national consciousness.

After independence, the Nigerian press expands rapidly. Newspapers multiply. Media houses grow in size and influence.

Editing becomes more structured:

Newsrooms develop formal processes

Sub-editors handle copy refinement

Editorial standards begin to stabilise

This period represents growth and institutionalisation. Editing is now part of an organised media system, not just individual effort.

Then comes the military era.

Here, the story takes a more complex turn.

Editing operates under pressure. Censorship becomes a reality. Certain stories cannot be published. Certain words cannot be used. Editors must navigate risk carefully.

In this context, editing becomes:

Strategic

Cautious

Sometimes coded

A headline, a phrase, or even the placement of a story can carry hidden meaning. Editors learn to communicate within constraints.

This period teaches an important lesson:

Editing is not always free. It is shaped by political and institutional power.

Now consider the contemporary era.

Nigeria’s media landscape has transformed again. Digital platforms have disrupted traditional publishing. Anyone with a smartphone can produce and distribute content.

Editing now faces new challenges:

Speed over accuracy

Viral content over verified information

Audience pressure and algorithmic influence

At the same time, opportunities have expanded:

Independent media platforms are emerging

Investigative journalism is gaining visibility

Diverse voices are entering public discourse

Editing today must balance:

Professional standards

Technological tools

Audience expectations

Concrete Examples to Reinforce
The growth of national newspapers reflects institutional maturity

The rise of independent digital platforms reflects democratisation of media

Social media reflects decentralisation of editorial control

Editing in Nigeria is not just a technical process. It is shaped by:

Editing Exercise: News Paragraph
Poorly Written Paragraph

The federal government yesterday announce that they is planning to introduce new education policy that will improve student performance across nigeria, the minister of education said the policy are focus on digital learning and teacher trainings but many school does not have computer and some teachers is not properly train which make the policy difficult to implement and students in rural area is mostly affected by this challenges.

Task 1: Identify Errors
Students should be able to identify the following:

1. Grammar and Agreement Errors
“announce” → incorrect tense

“they is planning” → subject-verb disagreement

“policy are focus” → incorrect structure

“school does not” → singular/plural mismatch

“teachers is” → subject-verb disagreement

“area is” → should be plural agreement

“this challenges” → incorrect determiner

2. Capitalisation Errors
“nigeria” → should be Nigeria

3. Punctuation Issues
Entire paragraph is a run-on sentence

Missing full stops to separate ideas

4. Clarity and Structure Issues
Ideas are not logically separated

Poor sentence flow

Lack of coherence between policy announcement and implementation challenges

5. Style Issues (Journalistic Writing)
Wordiness and repetition

Weak phrasing: “yesterday announce” instead of “announced yesterday”

Lack of precision and sharpness expected in news writing

Task 2: Improved Version
Edited Paragraph

The Federal Government yesterday announced plans to introduce a new education policy aimed at improving student performance across Nigeria.

The Minister of Education stated that the policy focuses on digital learning and teacher training. However, many schools lack adequate computer facilities, and several teachers are not properly trained, making implementation difficult.

Students in rural areas are expected to be the most affected by these challenges.

Teaching Note (How to Use This in Class)
First, let students attempt corrections individually

Then compare responses in pairs or small groups

Finally, present the refined version and explain why each change matters

Key Learning Outcome:
Students begin to see that editing is not just about correcting grammar, but about improving clarity, structure, and meaning.

SLIDE 14: Case Illustration
Title: Example: Same Story, Different Meaning
Scenario (Context for Students)
A report states that the Federal Government has approved a new education policy aimed at improving digital learning in public schools. However, funding and infrastructure challenges remain.

Version A: Neutral Headline
“FG Approves New Education Policy to Improve Digital Learning in Public Schools”

Version B: Sensational Headline
“FG Rolls Out New Education Policy Despite Lack of Computers in Schools”

Discussion Prompt
How does editing change audience perception?

Explainer (For Classroom Delivery)
Both headlines are based on the same underlying story. No facts have been changed. Yet, the interpretation shifts significantly.

Version A presents the policy as progress. It is forward-looking and institutional.

Version B foregrounds the limitation. It introduces doubt and potential criticism.

This demonstrates a critical principle:

Editing is not just about correctness. It is about framing.

The editor decides:

What to emphasise

What to downplay

What the audience notices first

Deeper Analytical Insight
Version A aligns with official narrative framing

Version B aligns with accountability or critical journalism framing

Both are valid within journalistic practice, but they serve different purposes and shape public reaction differently.

SLIDE: AI in Editing – Opportunities and Limitations
Title: AI Tools in Editing: Assistance, Not Replacement
What AI Can Do Well
Grammar and Language Correction
Identifies spelling, punctuation, and basic grammatical errors

Clarity and Readability Improvement
Suggests simpler sentence structures and improved flow

Speed and Efficiency
Processes large volumes of text quickly

Basic Content Refinement
Rewrites sentences for tone and consistency

Headline Suggestions
Generates multiple headline options for comparison

Practical Examples of Tools
Grammarly: grammar and style correction

ChatGPT: rewriting, summarisation, headline drafting

Microsoft Word Editor: basic proofreading and suggestions

What AI Cannot Reliably Do
Fact-Checking with Certainty
May generate or accept inaccurate information

Contextual Judgement
Struggles with nuance, cultural sensitivity, and local realities

Editorial Decision-Making
Cannot determine what should or should not be published

Ethical Reasoning
Does not understand consequences of misinformation

Meaning Construction
Cannot fully grasp how framing affects public perception

Key Risks
Over-reliance leading to reduced human critical thinking

Subtle errors that appear correct but are misleading

Loss of editorial voice and originality

Potential bias embedded in AI-generated suggestions

Core Principle
AI assists the editor, but the editor remains responsible for accuracy, judgement, and meaning.

Quick Class Prompt
Have you ever used AI to write or edit something?

Did you trust the output completely? Why or why not?

Lecturer Note
Keep this brief and practical:

Demonstrate one quick example (before vs AI-edited version)

Ask students to identify what AI improved and what it missed

Takeaway
AI improves speed and surface-level quality,
but human editors ensure truth, context, and credibility.
