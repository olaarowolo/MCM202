SEGMENT TWO: CORE TEACHING — PART 1
"The Newsroom Workflow: Five Stages Between Event and Audience" [4-5 minutes]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let me ask you something. [PAUSE] Imagine a breaking news event is happening
right now, right where you are. [beat] How does that information get from a
reporter's notebook to your phone screen? What processes does it pass through
before it gets to you? [beat] Does it get there by magic? [PAUSE]

Of course not. [ENERGETIC] News is not magic. It is a // structured process //.
[PAUSE]

We call it the newsroom workflow. [SLOW DOWN] The newsroom workflow is the
structured process through which news content is created, reviewed, and
published. And it is built around one non-negotiable standard: that what
reaches you is original, correct, accurate, clear, and timely. [PAUSE]

There are five stages. Let me take you through each one.

[ENERGETIC] Stage one: // News Gathering //. This is where it all begins. The
journalist goes out into the world — they attend events, conduct interviews,
analyze documents, and collect information from sources. The challenges at
this stage are real: incomplete information, tight time frames, the need to
filter out personal bias even as events are unfolding. What emerges from this
stage is raw material. It is not yet a news story. [PAUSE]

Stage two: // Drafting //. The journalist writes an initial version. At this
stage, they focus on getting facts down correctly, structuring the information
logically, and reaching for clarity. The common problems here are grammar
errors, poor structure, and missing context. [beat] The reporter is not
expected to be perfect. [PAUSE] That is exactly why stage three exists.

Stage three: // Sub-Editing //. [SLOW DOWN] This is the first major control
stage. The sub-editor takes the reporter's draft and reviews it, refines it,
checks it. We will spend the bulk of this episode on this stage — because
this is where most of the editorial work happens.

Stage four: // Editorial Review //. The senior editor reviews what the
sub-editor has passed up. This is the final control stage — focused on
alignment with editorial policy, legal risk, ethical standards, and news value.
Here is the critical reality: the editor's trust in the sub-editor is
significant. If something slips past the sub-editor, it is likely to make it
through. [beat] Which makes the sub-editor // the most important checkpoint //
in the entire process. [PAUSE]

Stage five: // Publishing //. The content is released to the audience. Print,
broadcast, digital, online — depending on the medium and the platform. And
this is where the story reaches your phone. [SLOW DOWN] Errors at this stage
affect public trust. You cannot recall a published story the way you recall a
product from a shelf. The damage is immediate, visible, and lasting. Whatever
was not caught before this stage is now part of the public record. [PAUSE]

So when I say: Reporter → Sub-editor → Editor → Publication — I am not asking
you to memorize a diagram. I am asking you to understand a system of
// accountability //. Built specifically because real mistakes have real
consequences. [PAUSE]

SEGMENT THREE: CORE TEACHING — PART 2
"The Sub-Editor: Role, Responsibilities, and Skills" [4-5 minutes]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

So who exactly is the sub-editor? [PAUSE]

A sub-editor is a professional responsible for refining content before
publication. They sit between the reporter and the editor — the critical
linkage in the newsroom chain. When a reporter finishes writing and hands over
copy, the first person they hand it to is the sub-editor. [PAUSE]

Think of the sub-editor as the quality control officer of the newsroom. Not
the most visible person. Not the one whose name appears on the byline. But the
most important checkpoint. [PAUSE]

Their core responsibilities cover six areas. [ENERGETIC]

One: // Edit for grammar and clarity //. Ensure the writing is clean, sentences
are clear, meaning is not lost in structure. But as we just established — this
is important, not first.

Two: // Fact-check content //. Verify statistics, confirm names and titles,
cross-check dates and places. This is at the heart of sub-editing.

Three: // Ensure consistency with style guides //. Every serious media
organization has a house style — rules about capitalization, how to handle
numbers, how to abbreviate, which titles take capital letters and which do not.
The sub-editor enforces this standard across all content, all writers, every
day. [PAUSE]

Four: // Write headlines and captions //. In many newsrooms, the sub-editor
writes the headline. That is an enormous responsibility. The headline is what
most readers see first. And as we discussed in Episode 1, the headline is not
neutral. It is a frame. The sub-editor who writes a headline is making an
editorial decision about how millions of readers will interpret a story. [PAUSE]

Five: // Detect legal and ethical risks //. Before a story reaches the editor,
the sub-editor must ask: could this expose the organization to a defamation
claim? Does naming this person this way cause unjustified harm? Are there
ethical violations — confidentiality breaches, sensationalism disguised as
news, exploitative use of imagery? These are questions that belong at this
stage, not after publication. [PAUSE]

Six: // Quality assurance on the complete piece //. The sub-editor is the
last person who can stop a bad story before it reaches the final decision-
maker. Their job is to ensure that what goes up is worth publishing. [PAUSE]

Now — what makes a sub-editor genuinely excellent? [SLOW DOWN]

Four qualities define this. First: // attention to detail // — an uncommon,
almost relentless attention to what is actually on the page versus what should
be. Second: // strong language skills // — because you cannot fix what you
cannot see. Third: // critical thinking // — questioning not just grammar
but logic, argument structure, and evidence. And fourth: // ethical awareness //
— understanding that every editorial decision carries consequences beyond the
page. [PAUSE]

None of these are glamorous. But all of them are essential. [beat] If you are
heading into journalism or media, the sub-editor's desk is where editorial
instinct is built. Do not underestimate it. Do not skip it.

[NEWSROOM SNAPSHOT — Nigerian context]
[~90 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

// Newsroom Snapshot //. [PAUSE]

The copy arrives. A reporter has filed a story about a new federal government
education policy. The opening sentence reads: "The federal government yesterday
announce that they is planning to introduce a new education policy that will
improve student performance across Nigeria." [PAUSE]

You are the sub-editor. Track Changes is on. You begin. [ENERGETIC]

First issue: "announce" should be "announced" — wrong tense in a past-tense
report. Second: "they is planning" — subject-verb disagreement and an awkward
construction. Change to "plans to introduce." Third: "federal government" as a
proper noun requires capital letters — Federal Government. Three issues in
one sentence. [PAUSE]

Second paragraph: "The Minister of Education stated that the policy are focused
on digital learning and teacher training." Another error: "the policy are
focused" — pick either "the policy focuses" or "the policies focus" and apply
it consistently throughout the story. Fourth issue caught. [PAUSE]

Final paragraph: "Students in rural area is mostly affected by these challenges."
Two problems: "area" should be "areas" — and "is" should be "are." Fifth and
sixth issues. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] This is the work. This is literally what a sub-editor does with
their time. Not glamorous. Not visible. But // necessary //. Because if any of
those errors reach publication, the organization looks careless. And careless
organizations lose trust. [PAUSE]

This paragraph is a real teaching example from MCM 202 practical sessions. That
means these errors were written, submitted, and needed to be caught. The
question is always the same: would you catch all of them? Every time?

SEGMENT FOUR: CORE TEACHING — PART 3
"Digital Editing: Track Changes and What It Replaces" [4-5 minutes]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

For most of journalism's history, editing happened on paper. With a pen. With
a set of standardized marks — symbols that every editor and typesetter could
read and understand. [PAUSE]

These editing symbols are not arbitrary. They are a // shared language //. A
stroke through a letter means delete. The caret — the ^ sign — means insert
something here. A circle around two letters means transpose their order. When
every editor in a newsroom uses the same symbols, the communication between
them is precise, fast, and unambiguous. [PAUSE]

We will cover the full editing symbol set in detail in Episode 5. But for now,
understand this: their purpose was always the same. // Clear, unambiguous
correction instructions //. Same purpose. Same principle. Right up to today.
[PAUSE]

Today, we edit digitally. Microsoft Word. Google Docs. And the tool that has
largely replaced the red pen is // Track Changes //. [SLOW DOWN]

Here is how it works. Open your document. Go to the Review tab at the top.
Click Track Changes — toggle it on. When it is active, every change you make is
recorded. Deleted text appears with a strikethrough. Inserted text is
highlighted. Every correction is attributed to the person who made it, with a
time stamp. [PAUSE]

The original author — or the senior editor — can then go through those
changes one by one. They can accept each one. They can reject each one. Or they
can accept all changes at once. [beat] The document becomes a // conversation //
between the reporter and the sub-editor. A professional record of editorial
decisions. [PAUSE]

You have four viewing options you should know. [ENERGETIC] // All Markup //
shows every tracked change in full. // Simple Markup // shows a clean version
with small flags indicating where changes exist. // No Markup // shows what the
finished piece looks like if all changes are accepted. And // Original // shows
you the raw draft before any editing was done. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] Knowing how to switch between these views is practical. Reading
the piece in No Markup mode after making your edits gives you a clean final
read — and that second read usually catches the error your first pass missed.
[PAUSE]

This is not just a software skill. In any professional newsroom — print,
digital, broadcast production — fluency with Track Changes is expected. Learn
it now, before you need it in a professional setting. [PAUSE]


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
[SYMBOL CHECK — recurring segment]
[~45 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

// Symbol Check //. [PAUSE]

Today's symbol: the // caret // — written as ^. In manuscript editing, the
caret means // insert //. You place it at the point in the text where
something needs to be added, and write what should be inserted above or beside
it. [PAUSE]

Example: if a sentence reads "The government education launched plan" and a
caret is placed between "government" and "education" with the word "new"
written above it — the corrected sentence reads "The government launched a new
education plan." [PAUSE]

In digital editing, Track Changes handles insertions automatically. But when
working on printed manuscripts or typewritten copy, the caret remains the
standard insertion marker. Know it. Use it correctly. [PAUSE]

The full symbol set builds in Episode 5.

A — Correct all the grammar errors throughout the article
B — Rewrite the headline to make it more engaging
C — Verify the statistical claims against credible sources
D — Check the word count and trim the article to length

[PAUSE] Hold your answer. Think about what the sub-editor's core responsibility

The answer is // C — verify the statistical claims //. [PAUSE] Because

RECAP
[~60 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Three things to take from this episode. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] One: The newsroom workflow has five stages — news gathering,
drafting, sub-editing, editorial review, and publishing. The order is not
arbitrary. It is a quality control system. And errors that survive the sub-
editing stage have a high probability of reaching your audience intact.

Two: The sub-editor is the critical control point of the newsroom. Their
responsibilities cover accuracy, clarity, consistency, legality, and ethical
safety. The single most essential quality they need is // attention to detail //.
Not glamorous. Essential.

Three: Digital editing today runs through Track Changes. The tools change. The
principle does not. Whether you hold a red pen or review a marked document on
screen — the sub-editor's job is to communicate corrections // clearly,
precisely, and consistently //. [PAUSE]

The newsroom depends on this. Your audience depends on this.
