SEGMENT TWO: CORE TEACHING — PART 1
"Advanced Editing: Moving Beyond Grammar to Truth" [4-5 minutes]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let me put a question to you. [PAUSE] Is a grammatically correct sentence
always a // good // sentence? [beat] Think about it honestly. [PAUSE]

You can have a sentence where every word is spelled correctly, every verb
agrees with its subject, every comma sits in the right place. [beat] And the
sentence can still be // dangerous //. [PAUSE] Because it might be factually
wrong. It might be biased. It might be ambiguous — meaning two entirely
different things depending on who reads it. [PAUSE]

// This is what advanced editing addresses. // [SLOW DOWN]

Advanced editing is the point where you stop correcting and start // deciding //.
You are not just asking: is this sentence grammatically correct? You are
asking: is this // true //? Is this // clear // for this specific audience? Is
this // fair // to everyone involved? Does it expose the organization to legal
or ethical risk? What does this frame suggest about the subject — and is that
the // right // frame for this context? [PAUSE]

These are editorial decisions. Not editorial corrections. And there is a real
difference. [ENERGETIC] Corrections fix what is broken. Decisions shape what
is // communicated //. [PAUSE]

The principles of accuracy rest on this foundation: every fact must be correct.
Names spelled and verified correctly. Dates confirmed. Figures checked. Locations
accurate. And all of it must be // verifiable // — meaning, if someone
challenges what you published, you can point immediately to a credible, named
source. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] A single inaccurate figure can destroy a publication's credibility.
Not weaken it. // Destroy // it. [PAUSE]

Here is a direct example. A story reads: "The Federal Government allocates
3 million naira to the education sector." [beat] Does that figure look correct?
[PAUSE] Now the story is revised: "The Federal Government allocates 3 billion
naira to the education sector." [beat] One letter. M and B. [PAUSE]

The scale of the figure has just shifted by a factor of one thousand. [beat]
And to a non-specialist reader, both sentences look normal. Both are
grammatically perfect. Both could slip past a distracted sub-editor on a
deadline. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] That is why accuracy is the // first priority // of advanced
editing. Not speed. Not style. [beat] // Accuracy //.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
[EDITOR'S EYE — recurring segment]
[~60 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

// Editor's Eye //. [PAUSE]

A story about a government education policy contains this sentence:
"According to reports, the policy has already improved student performance
in 50 countries." [PAUSE]

You are the editor. Flag every problem before I do. [beat] Run through it.
[PAUSE]

First: "According to reports" — which reports? Who published them? When? On
what data? This is unattributed. It proves nothing. [beat] Flag it.

Second: "50 countries" — this is a national government policy. How exactly
has it reached 50 countries? [beat] Extraordinary claims require extraordinary
evidence. [beat] Flag it.

Third: "already improved" — what is the evidence of improvement? What metric?
What baseline? Without that, the word "improved" is an empty assertion. [beat]
Flag it. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] Three editorial problems in one sentence. None of them grammar
issues. All three of them credibility crises waiting to happen. [PAUSE]

This is what editorial judgment looks like in practice. Not red pens.
// Judgment //.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
SEGMENT THREE: CORE TEACHING — PART 2
"Fact-Checking Techniques: How to Verify Before You Publish" [5-6 minutes]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

So how do you actually fact-check? [PAUSE] Here are five techniques every
editor must build into their workflow. [ENERGETIC]

Technique one: // Cross-check multiple sources //. Do not confirm a fact by
finding a single source that supports it. Find three. Find four. If they all
agree — you are getting closer to reliable. If they contradict — you have a
problem to resolve before publication. Never publish on one source alone.
[PAUSE]

Technique two: // Use official data sources //. For statistics, use government
data, academic research, established international organizations. Not social
media. Not summaries. [beat] The primary source. Go to the original document,
the original report, the original dataset. That is where your credibility is
anchored. [PAUSE]

Technique three — and this one carries real weight in today's environment:
// treat AI with caution //. [SLOW DOWN] AI tools have training cutoffs. They
have data gaps. They can state things confidently that are no longer true,
or were never true in your specific local context. [PAUSE]

Here is a real example. At a fact-checking conference in Africa, the question
was posed to a leading AI search tool: what is the biggest library in the
world? The AI gave a confident answer — and the answer was factually incorrect.
[beat] Confident. [beat] Incorrect. [beat] That is the trap. // Confidence is
not the same as accuracy. // [PAUSE]

Use AI as a starting point. A direction-finder. But your professional
verification does not end with an AI response. It begins there. [PAUSE]

Technique four: // confirm quotes and attribution //. When you quote a person,
you are giving your professional guarantee that those are their exact words —
or that any paraphrase is clearly labeled as such. Quote someone out of
context, alter their words slightly, or remove a qualifier that changes the
meaning — and you have not just made an error. You may have defamed them.
[PAUSE]

Technique five: // avoid assumptions //. This is the most common editorial
failure, and the hardest one to correct by instinct. Assumption is the lowest
form of knowledge. As an editor: if you do not know — // you verify //. You
do not guess. You do not assume it is probably accurate. [beat] You confirm.
[PAUSE]

Common categories of fact error to watch: // wrong figures // — especially
numbers that are easy to confuse (millions vs billions, percentages, dates).
// Misquoted statements // — particularly partial quotes that alter meaning.
// Incorrect names or titles // — Dr. where there is no doctorate, Senator
where they are a Representative. // Outdated information // — facts correct
six months ago but no longer true today. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] Every one of these is fixable before publication. Once published,
none of them are truly undone. A correction notice signals failure. Your
credibility is built slowly and lost quickly. [PAUSE]

[NEWSROOM SNAPSHOT — Nigerian context]
[~90 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

// Newsroom Snapshot //. [PAUSE]

An article arrives for editorial review. It is about Lagos State University.
The article makes three claims. [PAUSE]

One: LASU has been ranked the number one university in Africa. Two: the
university recently received 200 billion naira in federal funding. Three: 95%
of graduates secured employment within three months of graduation. [PAUSE]

Every single claim is attributed to "according to reports" With no named
source. No date. No organization. [PAUSE]

You are the editor. Before a word goes to layout — you flag all three. [ENERGETIC]

Claim one: ranked number one in Africa. [beat] Which organization issued this
ranking? When? Based on which criteria? If you cannot name it precisely, the
claim cannot stand as written. Change to "among leading universities in Africa,
according to [specific organization and year]" — or remove entirely until
properly sourced. [PAUSE]

Claim two: 200 billion naira in federal funding. [beat] Consider Nigeria's
total national education budget. This figure is not just unsourced — it is
economically implausible on its face. An editor who has context will recognize
this immediately. Verify, correct, or remove. [PAUSE]

Claim three: 95% employed within three months. [beat] Given current
unemployment figures in Nigeria, this claim requires strong, specific,
verifiable evidence — from the university's own published records, from the
National Bureau of Statistics, or from a named independent body. "Students have
confirmed" is not credible sourcing. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] This is the work. Identifying the most dangerous claims, flagging
missing verification points, challenging vague attributions. This happens
before layout. Before headline. Before publication. If it does not happen here
— these claims reach your audience as facts. [PAUSE]

And once they do — [beat] you own them.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
SEGMENT FOUR: CORE TEACHING — PART 3
"Style, Consistency, Ambiguity, and Removing Bias" [4-5 minutes]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

We have covered accuracy and fact-checking. Now the third dimension of
advanced editing. [PAUSE]

// Style and consistency //. [SLOW DOWN]

Style in editing means uniformity across content — and across time. When your
publication writes "FG" in one paragraph, "Nigerian Government" in the next,
and "Federal Government" in the paragraph after that — the reader experiences
inconsistency. And inconsistency reads as carelessness. Even when every fact
is technically correct, the absence of a house style standard signals a
disorganized editorial process. [PAUSE]

Key style elements to watch carefully. // Capitalization // — which titles and
institutions take capital letters and which do not. // Numbers and figures //
— do you write "ten" or "10"? Your house style resolves this. // Dates // —
"April 15, 2026" or "15 April 2026"? Both are correct. Neither is wrong. But
pick one and apply it everywhere. // Abbreviations // — standard English
places a full stop after each letter (M.C.M.). Some organizations strip the
full stops as a deliberate style choice. Know your organization's decision
and apply it consistently. [PAUSE]

Now — // ambiguity //. [SLOW DOWN]

Ambiguity is when a sentence can mean two different things, or fails to
specify what it is actually claiming. Here is an example: "Students discussed
issues with teachers." [PAUSE]

Read that again carefully. [beat] Who has the issues? The students? The
teachers? Both? What kind of issues? About what subject? About whom? [PAUSE]
As written, the sentence answers none of these. An editor must interrogate
it — and rewrite it with specificity. Replace "issues" with the actual subject.
Name the context. Give the reader what they need to understand. [PAUSE]

And now — // bias //. [ENERGETIC]

Bias in editing is when the language used in a story reveals the editor's
or publication's position on the subject, rather than reporting it fairly.
Here is a biased version of a sentence: "The government // failed // again
to fix education problems." [PAUSE]

The word // failed // is an editorial judgment. It is not a neutral reporting
word. It announces that the writer has already decided the government is
incompetent. The reader is not given the information and allowed to form their
own view — they are being told what to conclude. [PAUSE]

Here is the neutral version: "The government has // not resolved // ongoing
education challenges." [PAUSE]

Same situation. Same underlying facts. But now the reader receives the
information and draws the conclusion themselves. The editor has stepped back.
That is called // editorial neutrality //. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] Editorial neutrality does not mean you have no views. Opinion
journalism and investigative editorial work exist and serve important purposes.
But in // news reporting //, the editor's job is fair witnessing. Report.
Do not judge. Let the evidence speak. [PAUSE]

In practical terms: every time you choose a word that carries judgment — a
word like "failed," "accused," "refused to act," "ignored" — you are making
an editorial decision. Make it consciously. Know precisely when you are shaping
meaning and when you are simply reporting it. The difference between the two
is the difference between journalism and advocacy. [PAUSE]


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
[SYMBOL CHECK — recurring segment]
[~45 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

// Symbol Check //. [PAUSE]

Today's symbol: // stet //. [PAUSE] From the Latin meaning "let it stand."
[SLOW DOWN]

When an editor marks a correction on a manuscript — strikes through a word,
for example — and then changes their mind, they write // stet // beside the
marked correction. It signals: disregard what I marked. Restore the original.
[PAUSE]

In digital editing using Track Changes, rejecting a tracked change achieves
exactly the same function. The word is restored to its original form. The
editorial decision is reversed. [PAUSE]

But the concept matters beyond the mechanics. Knowing when to say // stet //
— knowing when to stop editing and preserve the original author's voice or
precise phrasing — is itself an act of editorial judgment. Not every marked
change should be accepted. [beat] Sometimes the original was right. [PAUSE]

The full symbol set continues in Episode 5.

CHECKPOINT QUESTION
[~60 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Checkpoint question for this episode. [PAUSE]

Which of the following is the // strongest indicator // that a claim in a
news report may be unreliable? [PAUSE]

A — The claim includes specific dates and named individuals
B — The claim is supported by an official government statement
C — The claim uses vague language such as "according to reports" without
 naming the source
D — The claim appears in both the headline and the body of the article

[PAUSE] Consider what we covered on attribution. Consider what "according to
reports" actually tells a reader — which is nothing. It names no source, no
date, no publication, no data methodology. [PAUSE]

The answer is // C //. [PAUSE] Vague attribution is the clearest editorial
red flag. It signals that the writer either did not verify the claim or could
not verify it — and relied on vagueness to pass unnoticed. A responsible
editor flags it immediately and sends the story back for proper sourcing.
[PAUSE]

On Option B: an official government statement is not automatically reliable
either. Official sources can be wrong, politically motivated, or outdated. But
they are named, attributable, and challengeable — which is a higher standard
than "according to reports." That is why B is not the weakest option here.

If you got this right — well done. If you chose A or D — re-listen to the
fact-checking techniques section of this episode before your next practical.

RECAP
[~60 seconds]
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Three things to take from this episode. [PAUSE]

[SLOW DOWN] One: Advanced editing is not about grammar. It is about accuracy,
truth, and fairness. A grammatically correct sentence can be factually
wrong, dangerously biased, or so ambiguous it communicates nothing clearly.
Editorial judgment is not just about what is broken — it is about what is
// true and defensible //.

Two: Fact-checking is not optional. Cross-check sources. Use primary and
official data. Confirm quotes precisely. Treat AI as a starting point, not a
final authority. Avoid assumptions. [beat] Your credibility is the sum of your
fact-checking decisions. Every published error costs more than you expect.

Three: Style consistency, eliminating ambiguity, and removing bias are not
cosmetic improvements. They are the editorial standards that define whether
your publication can be // trusted //. An editor who maintains these standards
protects the audience, the organization, and the profession. [PAUSE]

Editing is about grammar. But ultimately — it is about // truth //. [PAUSE]
Take that with you.
